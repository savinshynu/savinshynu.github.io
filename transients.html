Detection of Astrophysical Explosions through Image Processing

Introduction

Astrophysical explosions (a.k.a cosmic transients) are catastrophic events in space that release enormous amounts of energy. These explosions occur in various forms, each associated with different astrophysical processes includes events like supernovae, stellar flares, etc.

In this project, I developed a Python based (numpy, pandas, scipy, matplotlib, h5py) image processing pipeline to process large amounts of images from the 2 LWA radio telescopes (LWA1 and LWA-SV) in New Mexico to detect energetic events occurring in our universe. Basically, we track rapid changes in the intensity of different astronomical sources in the images on 5 second time scales. This work has resulted in a scientific publication: Link to the paper.

The pipeline utilized data collection, data cleaning, image processing (background subtraction, threshold-based detection, tracking of objects), classification of candidates, anomaly detection using images and time series analysis, and visualization of interesting new source candidates.

Data Collection

The study utilized over 120 TBs of images from the LWA radio telescope. The image data used in this study can be found at the LWA archive . The images are stored as big NumPy arrays of the shape (x-pixel, y-pixel, channels, time-steps) in a telescope specific format similar to HDF5 (having a header and data). Data Processing Pipeline

The python pipeline will feed the images and process the images to identify explosions. The different stages of image processing consist of:

  1. Data preprocessing:
    • Removed bad images affected by radio signal interference.
    • Masked different region of the images containing steady sources and regions affected by signal interference.
  2. Anomaly detection methodology:
    • Utilized image subtraction algorithm and threshold-based detection to identify explosions.
    • The pipeline subtracted the average of the previous six images from the current image to detect candidates.
    • The pixel regions in the images with intensities greater than a signal to noise ratio threshold of 6 are chosen as candidates.
    • Plot on right shows how the image of the sky looks after preprocessing and image subtraction.
  3. Classfication of Candidates:
    • Visualization of extracted image and time series analysis.
    • Probabilistic calculation and statistical measurements like signal to noise ratio, kurtosis of the time series to understand the significance.
    • Utilizing object tracking using mean shift of pixel intensities in images.
    • Finally, candidates are classified into true cosmic explosion, satellites, airplanes, radio signal disruption events, atmospheric disruptions and meteors.

Results

  • The study successfully detected a potential bright cosmic explosion on October 18, 2017, with a duration of 15-20 seconds. The plot below shows the image of the explosion detected and extracted time series from each station.

Conclusion

This project represents a significant step in the exploration of the cosmic explosion using radio telescopes and image processing. The detection of a potential cosmic event underscores the importance of continuous monitoring and the need for further studies to understand the nature and origin of these enigmatic sources.
